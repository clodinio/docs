# Preparing Your Recordings

The quality of your recording directly determines the quality of your transcript. This guide covers setup for common recording environments including interviews, focus groups, depositions, clinical sessions, and field research.

## General Principles

**Choose a quiet environment.** Background noise is the single biggest cause of transcription errors. HVAC units, open windows, traffic, keyboard noise, and ambient conversation all reduce accuracy. A quiet room with soft furnishings is significantly better than an open office or public space.

**Test before you record.** Make a 2-3 minute test recording before your actual session. Listen back through headphones to check that all voices are audible and that there is no unexpected noise. It takes three minutes and can prevent an unusable recording.

**Position your microphone well.** Built-in laptop or phone microphones are adequate for clear, single-speaker recordings, but they underperform for multi-speaker sessions and any recording more than a meter from the speaker. An external microphone makes a significant difference.

**Record at adequate volume.** If voices are too quiet, your transcriptionist has less to work with. Ask participants to speak at normal conversational volume and position microphones accordingly.

## Interview Recordings

**Equipment:**
- External USB or XLR microphone preferred over built-in
- For phone or video interviews: headset with built-in microphone, or dedicated audio interface
- For in-person interviews: cardioid microphone or lapel microphone

**Setup:**
- Position microphone 20-40cm from the primary speaker
- Face away from any noise sources (windows, air vents)
- Ask the interviewer and participant to introduce themselves at the start
- Disable any active noise suppression features in your recording software; these can cut out quiet speech

**During the recording:**
- Avoid paper shuffling, keyboard typing, or movement noise near the microphone
- Note speaker changes in your order notes if the audio makes them hard to distinguish

## Focus Group Recordings

Multi-speaker recordings require more preparation than one-on-one interviews.

**Equipment:**
- Boundary (PZM) microphone placed centrally on the table covers a wide area effectively
- Omnidirectional microphone in the center of the group works well for small groups (4-6 people)
- For larger groups (8-12 people), multiple microphones or directional microphones per participant improve clarity
- Avoid relying on a single microphone at one end of the table

**Setup:**
- Arrange seating in a circle or around a table so all participants are at a similar distance from the microphone
- Position the microphone away from air conditioning vents and windows
- Test with all participants in the room before the session begins
- Have participants introduce themselves by name or role at the start of the session (this helps with speaker attribution)

**During the session:**
- Ask participants to avoid talking over each other where possible (they will, but the reminder helps)
- Note the seating arrangement in your order notes if you want accurate named speaker attribution

## Video Interviews (Zoom, Teams, Webex)

**Settings:**
- Enable cloud recording in Zoom or Teams settings before the session
- For Zoom: enable separate audio tracks if available; this records each participant's audio independently and substantially improves accuracy for multi-speaker transcription
- Disable "enhanced noise suppression" in your platform's audio settings; aggressive noise suppression cuts out quiet speech and creates gaps
- Ask participants to join from a quiet location and use headphones

**After the session:**
- Allow 15-30 minutes for the recording to process and appear in your cloud storage before importing

## Legal Proceedings and Depositions

**In-person depositions:**
- Use a court-quality digital audio recorder or a professional microphone appropriate for conference room use
- Position microphones to capture both examining counsel and the witness clearly
- For video depositions, ensure the audio track is adequate; video-only recordings with poor audio are significantly harder to transcribe
- Note in your order if the witness has a strong accent or speaks unusually quickly

**Remote depositions (Zoom):**
- Follow the video interview guidance above
- Enable separate audio tracks in Zoom to isolate each participant's audio
- If any participant has a poor connection, flag this in your order notes so the transcriptionist can give those sections additional attention

## Field Research and Ethnographic Recordings

Field environments introduce challenges not present in controlled settings: wind, ambient noise, multiple environmental sounds, and varying distances from the microphone.

**Equipment:**
- Use a recorder with a built-in windscreen or attach a foam windscreen
- A fur windscreen (deadcat) substantially reduces wind noise for outdoor recordings
- Lapel microphones (lavalier mics) clipped to participants reduce distance-related volume loss
- Choose a recorder with manual gain control so you can adjust for different acoustic environments

**Setup:**
- Shield your recorder from direct wind
- Position as close to speakers as is practical and culturally appropriate
- In community settings, position yourself to capture the primary speaker while minimizing background noise

**During recording:**
- Announce the location, date, and speaker names at the start if this is consistent with your research protocol
- Note any periods of significant noise or movement in your order notes

## What to Include in Your Order Notes

Good order notes make a direct, measurable difference to transcript quality. Include:

- Speaker names or roles (Interviewer, Respondent, Moderator, Patient, Witness)
- Any known audio challenges (background noise in a specific section, one participant speaks quietly)
- Technical terminology or specialized vocabulary your transcriptionist should expect
- The subject matter of the recording (helps with context and terminology)
- The language or dialect if non-standard
- Any sections that are particularly important and should receive extra attention
- Anonymization requirements if applicable
