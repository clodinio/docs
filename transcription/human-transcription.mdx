## Transcript Formats

**Clean Verbatim** removes filler words, false starts, and repetitions while preserving the full meaning and context of what was said. This is the default format and appropriate for most research, legal, and professional uses.

**Full Verbatim** captures every utterance exactly as spoken, including all filler words (um, uh, like, you know), sighs, stutters, laughter, incomplete sentences, and non-verbal sounds. Required for:

- Conversation analysis and discourse analysis
- Legal depositions where every utterance is part of the record
- Psychotherapy and behavioral health research
- Linguistic studies where speech patterns are part of the data

A **hybrid** option is also available: filler words removed, but significant non-verbal cues (laughter, long pauses, emotional breaks, notable silences) retained. This works well for personal interviews and focus groups where emotional tone is analytically relevant without cluttering the text.

## Speaker Labels

Each participant is identified and labeled consistently throughout the transcript. Labels can be role-based (Interviewer, Respondent, Moderator, Participant 1, Witness, Counsel) or named if you provide a speaker list in your order notes.

Speaker labels are included as standard and are strongly recommended for focus groups, depositions, hearings, advisory boards, and any recording involving more than two participants.

## Accuracy Guarantee

We maintain a 99%\+ accuracy standard for clearly recorded audio. Clearly recorded means distinguishable speakers, minimal background noise, adequate recording volume, and clear speech.

If you receive a transcript that falls below this standard, notify us within 7 business days with your order number and specific examples with timecodes. We will conduct a quality review and provide a free correction for confirmed errors.

## Order Notes

The order notes field is one of the most effective tools for improving transcript quality. Use it to provide:

- Speaker names and roles
- Technical terminology, drug names, or specialized vocabulary
- Subject matter context (the topic of the interview, the research focus)
- Known audio challenges (background noise, heavy accents, speaking speed)
- A glossary of expected terms

The more context your transcriptionist has, the better the output.
